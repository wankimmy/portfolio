# Investor Prep Playbook

## When to use
- Pre-seed or seed fundraise preparation
- Accelerator / incubator application
- Strategic investor or follow-on round conversations
- Board materials or investor update preparation
- Preparing for due diligence from interested investors

## Prerequisites
- Problem, solution, and target customer are clearly defined
- Some evidence of demand (even qualitative) exists
- Founder(s) background and relevant experience documented
- Financial model or projections available (even rough)

## Phase 1: Narrative Foundation

**Goal:** Get the story right before building any slides.

**The core narrative (answer each in 1–2 sentences):**
1. **Problem:** What is broken, for whom, and how painful is it?
2. **Root cause:** Why hasn't it been solved before?
3. **Solution:** What do you do, and how is it different?
4. **Why now:** What changed (technology, regulation, behavior) that makes this the right moment?
5. **Why you:** What unfair advantage does this founding team have?
6. **Market:** How big is the opportunity, and how do you size it credibly?
7. **Traction:** What evidence exists that this is working?
8. **Ask:** How much, for what milestones, over what timeline?

**Narrative quality gates:**
- Problem is felt by a real human, not a company ("a VP of Sales struggles to..." not "enterprises need...")
- Solution is specific, not aspirational ("we do X" not "we enable X")
- Market sizing bottoms-up from customers, not top-down TAM quotes
- "Why now" has a named recent forcing function (not "AI is transforming everything")
- "Why you" is specific to this problem (not just a CV summary)

## Phase 2: Deck Construction

**Recommended slide order (10–12 slides, < 20 minutes):**

```
1. Cover           — Company name, tagline (one sentence)
2. Problem         — The pain, made vivid with 1–2 concrete examples
3. Solution        — What you built and how it works (demo or visual)
4. Why Now         — The forcing function that creates the window
5. Market Size     — Bottom-up + top-down, stated conservatively
6. Business Model  — How you make money, pricing model
7. Traction        — Key metrics, growth rate, customer logos or quotes
8. Competition     — Honest 2x2 or feature matrix (do not ignore competitors)
9. Team            — Relevant experience + why this team for this problem
10. Financials     — Revenue, runway, key assumptions (3-year P&L)
11. The Ask        — Round size, use of funds, milestones to hit
12. Appendix       — Deep-dives on market, tech, unit economics
```

**Slide design rules:**
- One idea per slide
- No paragraph text — bullet points or visuals only
- Every number has a source or methodology note
- Traction chart shows absolute numbers AND growth rate
- Competition slide names real competitors (dodging = red flag)

## Phase 3: Financial Model Prep

**Minimum credible financials for seed:**
- 3-year P&L with monthly detail for Year 1
- Revenue built bottom-up from: unit count × price × conversion rate
- Key assumptions explicitly stated (not hidden in formulas)
- Burn rate and cash runway visible
- Use of funds tied to milestones (e.g., "12 months runway, hire 2 engineers, reach $X ARR")

**Unit economics (if applicable):**
- CAC (customer acquisition cost) — blended and by channel
- LTV (lifetime value) — average contract × gross margin × retention
- LTV:CAC ratio — target ≥ 3:1 at scale
- Payback period — target < 18 months for SaaS

**Common financial model mistakes:**
- Hockey-stick with no inflection explanation
- "Even if we capture 1% of the market..." (top-down TAM reasoning)
- Expenses that don't scale with revenue
- Forgetting payroll taxes, benefits, infrastructure costs
- No sensitivity analysis on key assumptions

## Phase 4: Investor Memo

**When to write a memo:** Long-form reasoning for sophisticated investors, follow-on rounds, or when a deck isn't the right format.

```markdown
## Investment Memo: <Company Name>

### Executive Summary (3 sentences)
<Problem + solution + why this team wins>

### Problem
<Deep-dive: who suffers, how much, what they do today>

### Solution
<Technical approach, IP/defensibility, product screenshots/video>

### Market
<TAM/SAM/SOM with methodology, market dynamics, timing>

### Business Model
<Revenue model, pricing rationale, unit economics>

### Traction
<Key metrics, trend, customer quotes, NPS if available>

### Competition
<Named competitors, honest assessment, your wedge>

### Team
<Relevant experience, domain expertise, team gaps and plan>

### Financials
<3-year model summary, use of funds, milestones>

### Risks and Mitigations
<Top 3–5 risks with honest mitigation plans>

### The Ask
<Round size, terms (if applicable), close timeline>
```

## Phase 5: Q&A Preparation

**Questions every investor will ask — prepare concrete answers:**

| Question | What They're Really Asking |
|----------|---------------------------|
| Why will you win? | Do you have an unfair advantage or are you just smart? |
| What's your go-to-market? | Can you acquire customers efficiently? |
| Who else is doing this? | Are you aware of your competitive landscape? |
| What does your churn look like? | Is the product sticky or does it leak value? |
| Why now? | Why hasn't this been solved already? |
| What do you do with the money? | Are you capital-efficient and milestone-oriented? |
| What's your biggest risk? | Are you self-aware and do you have a plan? |
| How did you get your first 10 customers? | Can you sell without a brand? |
| What's your CAC / LTV? | Do you understand your unit economics? |
| What happens if [Big Co] builds this? | What's your defensibility? |

**Red flags to avoid in answers:**
- "We don't really have competitors" — investors know this is false
- Vague market size without methodology
- "If we just get 1% of the market..." as a market-size argument
- Not knowing your own metrics (burn rate, churn, CAC)
- Inconsistent numbers between deck and model

## Phase 6: Due Diligence Readiness

**Data room contents for seed:**
```
/data-room/
  company/
    cap_table.pdf
    incorporation_documents.pdf
    existing_investor_agreements.pdf (if any)
  financials/
    financial_model.xlsx
    bank_statements_last_3_months.pdf
    revenue_breakdown.xlsx (if applicable)
  product/
    product_demo_video.mp4
    technical_architecture.pdf (optional)
  customers/
    customer_list_anonymized.xlsx
    sample_contract_redacted.pdf
    reference_customers.md (3–5 contacts)
  team/
    founder_bios.pdf
    key_employee_agreements.pdf
  legal/
    IP_assignment_agreements.pdf
    any_outstanding_litigation.md
```

**Pre-due-diligence checklist:**
- [ ] Cap table is clean and correctly structured
- [ ] IP is fully assigned to the company (not a founder personally)
- [ ] No side agreements with early customers that cap pricing or grant equity
- [ ] No outstanding IP disputes or litigation
- [ ] All founders have signed IP and NDA agreements with the company

## Common Pitfalls

**Leading with the solution, not the problem** — investors fund the problem. Start with why the problem is severe and real before explaining what you built.

**Underestimating the competition slide** — a blank or dismissive competition section signals naivety. Name competitors, be honest about their strengths, and be specific about your wedge.

**Vanity metrics** — registered users, app downloads, and page views without engagement or revenue signal a weak story. Lead with metrics that predict retention and revenue.

**Asking for money without clear milestones** — "18 months runway" is not a milestone plan. "Reach $X ARR, hire 2 engineers, launch Y feature" is.

**Not knowing your numbers** — founders who can't recite burn rate, runway, CAC, and LTV in conversation signal they don't run a tight ship.

**Generic "Why you"** — "we're passionate about this problem" is noise. "We spent 5 years at X building exactly this infrastructure, so we have an 18-month technical head start" is a real answer.
